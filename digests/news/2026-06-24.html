1. IAEA chief, Trump says Iran agreed to nuclear inspections, Tehran denies deal - report

The Jerusalem Post · Israeli · right-leaning

What happened: Trump and the IAEA chief said Iran has agreed to allow long-term nuclear inspections following the US-Iran framework deal, but Tehran's foreign ministry flatly denied making any new commitments after the Switzerland talks. The dispute unfolded alongside friction over US naval positioning near the Strait of Hormuz.

Background: The claim follows a framework agreement to end the roughly four-month US-Iran war, with negotiators pushing for a final deal within 60 days and inspectors said to be invited back after Iranian nuclear sites were bombed.

Why it matters: Whether Iran actually accepts intrusive inspections of its bombed, 60%-enriched-uranium stockpile is the linchpin of the entire ceasefire and determines if the war stays ended.

Different views: Jerusalem Post and US officials frame it as Iran conceding to inspections and a $300bn 'peace deal,' while Al Jazeera stresses Iranian denials and 'no new commitments'; Iranian voices (via JPost) cast the MoU as a US 'declaration of defeat,' and the Guardian's commentary frames Trump's victory claims as a self-made catastrophe.

2. Israel continues to commit genocide by targeting children in Gaza, UN inquiry finds

The Guardian · UK · left-leaning

What happened: An independent UN commission of inquiry concluded that Israel is committing genocide in Gaza by deliberately targeting Palestinian children, saying the killings undermine the capacity of the Palestinian people to exist. Israel rejected the report as a 'libellous sham.'

Background: The three-member expert panel examined violations against Palestinian children since the start of the Gaza war, extending findings into the occupied West Bank.

Why it matters: A formal UN genocide finding adds legal and diplomatic weight to international pressure on Israel and could shape war-crimes proceedings and allied governments' policies.

Different views: The Guardian and BBC lead with the inquiry's genocide conclusion as the central fact, while the Jerusalem Post foregrounds Israel's rebuttal and frames the report as accusations 'prompting a strong Israeli rebuttal'; pro-Palestinian outlets present the targeting of children as established.

3. Israeli troops kill two in south Lebanon after lull in fighting, authorities say

BBC Middle East · UK · mainstream

What happened: Israeli troops killed two men in south Lebanon after a lull in fighting. Israel's military said the men were Hezbollah operatives, while the Iran-backed group accused Israel of violating the ceasefire.

Background: The killings come as US-brokered talks advance a plan for the Lebanese army to take over zones vacated by Israel after a Hezbollah withdrawal, with Iran treating a Lebanon ceasefire as a condition in its MoU with Washington.

Why it matters: Renewed lethal incidents test the fragile Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire and the broader US effort to stabilize Lebanon as part of the regional de-escalation track.

Different views: BBC neutrally presents both Israel's 'Hezbollah operatives' claim and Hezbollah's ceasefire-violation accusation; the Jerusalem Post frames coverage around the US-backed Lebanese army takeover plan and Hezbollah's demand that Israel withdraw, while pro-Palestinian framing emphasizes Israeli incursions (e.g. soldiers in Lebanese homes).
