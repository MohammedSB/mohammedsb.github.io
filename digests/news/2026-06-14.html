1. Trump says Iran peace deal could be signed by Sunday, with strait of Hormuz to open shortly after

The Guardian

What happened: Trump announced in a Truth Social post that the US will sign a new agreement with Iran the following day (Sunday), claiming it would block Tehran from obtaining a nuclear weapon and reopen the Strait of Hormuz to international shipping. He warned he reserves an 'ultimate alternative' if Iran refuses to sign. Tehran has disputed the timeline and said no exact date is set.

Background: The deal would mark the first stage of ending a war between the US/Israel and Iran now in its 107th day, with the destruction of Iran's enriched uranium reportedly central to negotiations.

Why it matters: A signed agreement could end a months-long regional war, reopen a critical global energy chokepoint, and reshape the nuclear standoff — but conflicting US-Iran messaging signals the deal remains fragile.

2. Israel attacks Beirut on same day Trump says Iran deal to be signed

Al Jazeera English

What happened: Israel bombed the southern suburbs of Beirut, saying it targeted Hezbollah 'infrastructure' in response to alleged Hezbollah drone fire. The strikes occurred on the same day Trump declared a US-Iran peace deal would be signed.

Background: Iran insists Lebanon must be included in any peace deal with the US, and Hezbollah remains a key Iran-aligned actor in the regional conflict.

Why it matters: Striking Beirut as a peace deal is announced underscores how unresolved the broader conflict remains; Tehran cited the attack as evidence the US lacks the will or ability to fulfil its commitments.

3. Growing risk that thousands buried in Gaza’s rubble may never be identified, says Red Cross

The Guardian

What happened: The International Committee of the Red Cross warned that the risk thousands of Palestinians buried beneath Gaza's rubble may never be identified is rising daily, as recovery efforts remain slow and many bodies have yet to be retrieved. The passing of time makes skeletonisation — and permanent loss of identification — increasingly likely.

Background: Gaza's devastated infrastructure and the scale of destruction have left vast numbers of dead unrecovered amid the ongoing humanitarian crisis.

Why it matters: It highlights the long-term human toll of the Gaza war beyond the immediate fighting, with implications for families, accountability, and the true death count.
