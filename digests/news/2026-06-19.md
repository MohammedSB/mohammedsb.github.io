### 1. [US-Iran talks to begin in Switzerland as Tehran says it has closed Hormuz Strait](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cwyekkwm1mmo?at_medium=RSS&at_campaign=rss)
*BBC Middle East · UK · mainstream*

**What happened:** US-Iran talks were set to begin in Switzerland, with delegations from both sides plus Pakistani and Qatari mediators, even as Tehran declared it had closed the Strait of Hormuz. The US military disputed Iran's claim that the strait was actually shut.

**Background:** The talks follow a recently signed US-Iran memorandum of understanding meant to end their conflict, but the deal remains fragile amid continued violence in Lebanon.

**Why it matters:** A durable US-Iran agreement would reshape Gulf security, sanctions, and the broader regional balance; its collapse risks renewed escalation across multiple fronts.

**Different views:** Al Jazeera and BBC frame the talks around Iran's Hormuz closure and Israeli strikes derailing diplomacy, while the Jerusalem Post emphasizes US negotiators (Witkoff, Vance) and casts Iran's moves as retaliation/brinkmanship; the Guardian focuses on what authoritarian lessons Iran's leadership may draw.


### 2. [Iran says it is closing strait of Hormuz over Israeli strikes in Lebanon](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/jun/20/israeli-strikes-southern-lebanon-reports-renewed-ceasefire)
*The Guardian · UK · left-leaning*

**What happened:** Iran announced it was closing the Strait of Hormuz in response to Israeli strikes in Lebanon, with the IRGC warning ships away from the waterway. It was unclear whether the closure had actually been carried out, and it threatened to derail the just-signed interim deal with the US.

**Background:** The Strait of Hormuz is the world's most critical oil chokepoint, and Iran has periodically threatened closure during past crises.

**Why it matters:** Any genuine closure would spike global energy prices and could trigger military confrontation, while also jeopardizing the nascent US-Iran agreement.

**Different views:** The Guardian and Al Jazeera present the closure as Iran's reaction to Israeli aggression in Lebanon and question whether it is real; the Jerusalem Post frames it as Iranian retaliation and stresses logistical fallout (80 mines, reopening possibly delayed to year's end), and one Al Jazeera op-ed warns it would turn Iran into a pariah.


### 3. [Lebanon says Israeli strikes kill 47 as Israel says four soldiers killed by Hezbollah](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c23ymz1n9rmo?at_medium=RSS&at_campaign=rss)
*BBC Middle East · UK · mainstream*

**What happened:** Lebanon said Israeli strikes killed 47 people in one of the deadliest days since March, while Israel said four of its soldiers were killed by Hezbollah. The two sides then agreed to renew a fragile ceasefire, though Israel said it would continue operating in southern Lebanon.

**Background:** The flareup came a day after the US and Iran signed a deal meant to end their conflict, including the fighting in Lebanon, but the truce repeatedly frayed.

**Why it matters:** Continued Israel-Hezbollah clashes threaten to unravel the wider US-Iran agreement and risk reigniting full-scale war in Lebanon.

**Different views:** Al Jazeera and the Guardian foreground Lebanese civilian deaths and Israeli occupation/destruction of villages; the BBC reports both the 47 Lebanese killed and four Israeli soldiers; the Jerusalem Post centers Israeli soldier casualties, Hezbollah drone attacks, and US intelligence warnings that Israeli operations could undermine the MoU, with Hezbollah's chief vowing his group can still win.
