1. US-Iran talks strained as Trump threats spark Iranian walkout

The Guardian · UK · left-leaning

What happened: The first round of direct US-Iran negotiations opened in Lucerne, Switzerland, mediated by Pakistan and Qatar, aiming to constrain Iran's nuclear program within a 60-day window. The opening was tense: Iranian negotiators briefly walked out in protest at Donald Trump's social-media threats to bomb Iran and 'kidnap' the negotiating team, but talks are set to continue through the week, with both sides agreeing to form a high-level oversight committee.

Background: The talks follow a recent war and an interim agreement signed days earlier; Lebanon and Israeli strikes there remain a major unresolved flashpoint threatening the process.

Why it matters: A durable US-Iran nuclear deal would reshape regional security, sanctions, and the balance of power, but the fragile, threat-laden start shows how easily it could collapse.

Different views: Vance and the Jerusalem Post emphasize a 'good foundation' and amicable conclusion, Iranian and Qatari/Pakistani statements stress 'progress,' while the Guardian foregrounds Trump's threats and the Iranian walkout, and JPost analysis frames Iran's parallel moves (Hormuz, Lebanon) as leverage and bad faith.

2. Iran says it is closing strait of Hormuz over Israeli strikes in Lebanon

The Guardian · UK · left-leaning

What happened: Iran announced it was closing the Strait of Hormuz in response to waves of Israeli strikes in Lebanon, with the IRGC warning ships not to approach. Vessel traffic plunged sharply — five ships passed on Sunday versus 26 the day before — and maritime bodies warn roughly 80 mines must be cleared, with normal traffic possibly not resuming until year's end.

Background: Roughly a fifth of global oil passes through Hormuz; Iran has periodically threatened closure, and over 40 minelaying vessels were reportedly destroyed earlier this month.

Why it matters: A sustained closure threatens global energy supplies and could derail the just-signed US-Iran interim deal, directly linking the Lebanon conflict to world markets.

Different views: The Jerusalem Post frames the closure as Iran weaponizing Hormuz as 'strategic leverage' and notes Europe being sidelined, while Al Jazeera and the Guardian present it more as Iran's reaction to Israeli strikes in Lebanon.

3. Israel will not withdraw troops from southern Lebanon ahead of negotiations, official tells 'Post'

The Jerusalem Post · Israeli · right-leaning

What happened: An Israeli official told the Jerusalem Post that Israel will not withdraw troops from southern Lebanon ahead of negotiations, contradicting an earlier LBCI report that the US had told Lebanon a partial 'goodwill' withdrawal was planned. Defense Minister Israel Katz said there was 'no restriction' on Israeli soldiers eliminating threats in Lebanon, while Israeli strikes reportedly killed at least 20 people.

Background: Israel maintains a security zone in southern Lebanon under a fragile ceasefire; the US is simultaneously pressing to disarm Hezbollah and enforce the truce.

Why it matters: Israel's refusal to withdraw and continued strikes are the main wildcard threatening both the Lebanon ceasefire and the broader US-Iran track tied to it.

Different views: JPost analysis debates whether IDF soldiers are 'dying in vain' and frames operations around disarming Hezbollah; Hezbollah's Naim Qassem demands full Israeli withdrawal and accuses the IDF of war crimes; the Guardian highlights Palestinian and Lebanese civilian deaths from the strikes.
