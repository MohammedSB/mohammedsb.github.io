Top Stories

1. US and Iranian presidents sign deal aiming to end war

BBC Middle East

What happened: The presidents of the US and Iran signed a 14-paragraph memorandum of understanding aimed at ending their roughly four-month war. The agreement includes an end to fighting, a reopening of the strait of Hormuz, and a stated commitment that Iran will never possess a nuclear weapon.

Background: The deal follows the US-Israeli attack on Iran earlier in 2025 and months of fighting that closed the strait of Hormuz and roiled global oil markets.

Why it matters: It marks a formal attempt to end a major regional war, but leaves Iran's nuclear program and key terms to future negotiation, making the ceasefire's durability uncertain.

2. US-Iran talks abruptly called off after Israel and Hezbollah trade deadly attacks

The Guardian

What happened: US-Iran talks in Switzerland to implement the peace deal were abruptly cancelled after Hezbollah killed four Israeli soldiers and Israel responded with a wave of airstrikes across southern Lebanon and the Bekaa valley. JD Vance's staff were already at an airbase ready to fly to the summit when the trip was scrapped.

Background: The flare-up came just as Washington and Tehran were moving to cement the newly signed ceasefire, with Israel and Hezbollah trading deadly attacks despite the agreement.

Why it matters: The Lebanon front threatens to unravel the fragile US-Iran deal before it can be implemented, showing how regional proxy conflicts can derail the broader peace.

3. US-Iran deal leaves core sticking points unresolved - and a $300bn question

BBC Middle East

What happened: Analysis of the signed US-Iran agreement finds its core sticking points unresolved: although Trump insists Iran will never develop a nuclear weapon, the text falls short of guaranteeing that, and a reported $300bn redevelopment package for Iran remains contested.

Background: Trump cited Iran's nuclear program as the reason for starting the conflict, yet the memorandum defers the program's fate to further talks.

Why it matters: The gap between Trump's claims and the actual text raises doubts about whether the deal achieves US war aims and whether both sides can sustain it.
