1. US-Iran talks in Switzerland get under way as strait of Hormuz remains closed

The Guardian · UK · left-leaning

What happened: US and Iranian delegations began high-stakes talks at the Bürgenstock resort in Switzerland, mediated by Qatar and Pakistan, aiming to build out the fragile interim deal that ended their 110-day war. JD Vance said the talks seek progress on both the nuclear issue and the Lebanon ceasefire, with Witkoff and Araghchi meeting and an emergency session on Israel-Hezbollah added to the agenda.

Background: The talks follow a recently signed US-Iran Memorandum of Understanding ending direct hostilities; the process has been complicated by Iran's claimed closure of the Strait of Hormuz and ongoing Israeli strikes in Lebanon.

Why it matters: The outcome will determine whether the ceasefire holds and whether a durable agreement constraining Iran's nuclear program is reached, shaping regional stability and US engagement with the Muslim world.

Different views: Al Jazeera frames Iran as seeking US pressure on Israel to halt the Lebanon war; the Jerusalem Post centers Vance as a 'closer' and details Israeli anxieties and intelligence warnings; the Guardian emphasizes the deal's fragility and questions about Iran's post-war authoritarian turn.

2. Iran says it is closing strait of Hormuz over Israeli strikes in Lebanon

The Guardian · UK · left-leaning

What happened: Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps announced it was closing the Strait of Hormuz in retaliation for Israeli strikes in Lebanon, warning ships not to approach the strategic waterway. The US disputed that the strait is actually shut, and reports suggest as many as 80 mines may need clearing, with normal traffic possibly not resuming until year's end.

Background: The Strait of Hormuz is the world's most important oil chokepoint; Iran framed the move as the 'first step' in retaliation for alleged US and Israeli breaches of the new Memorandum of Understanding.

Why it matters: A genuine closure threatens global energy supplies and could derail the just-signed US-Iran deal, marking a major escalation despite the ostensible move toward peace.

Different views: Iranian outlets (via Mehr) present it as legitimate retaliation for violations; the BBC and Guardian highlight US denial that the strait is closed and ambiguity over whether the threat was executed; the Jerusalem Post stresses the mining and prolonged disruption, casting Iran's action as destabilizing and the talks as paused as a result.

3. Israeli strikes kill six people in Gaza including Al Jazeera cameraman, officials say

BBC Middle East · UK · mainstream

What happened: An Israeli strike killed six people in Gaza, including Al Jazeera cameraman Ahmed Wishah. The Israeli military accused Wishah of being a 'Hamas sniper operative' but provided no evidence, while Al Jazeera and press groups counted him among hundreds of journalists killed since October 2023.

Background: Wishah is described as at least the 12th Al Jazeera journalist and among 260+ Palestinian journalists killed during Israel's war on Gaza.

Why it matters: The killing renews scrutiny of press freedom and civilian harm in Gaza, and the unsubstantiated militancy allegation feeds an ongoing dispute over Israel's targeting of journalists.

Different views: Al Jazeera and the Guardian frame it as the targeted killing of a 'kind, principled' journalist among many press deaths; the BBC reports the killing while noting the IDF's evidence-free 'Hamas sniper' claim, foregrounding the contested justification.
