1. OpenMedQ: Broad Open Pretraining for Medical Vision-Language Models

Ibrahim Gulluk, Max Van Puyvelde, Olivier Gevaert  ·  2606.12953

TL;DR: OpenMedQ pretrains a medical vision-language model on the broadest fully-open multimodal medical mix to date, achieving state-of-the-art on PathVQA and VQA-MED while being far smaller than competing models.

Problem: Medical VLMs have been limited by narrow, proprietary pretraining data, making it hard to study the effect of broad multimodal coverage across radiology, pathology, and microscopy. Existing open models are outperformed by closed systems orders of magnitude larger.

Method: OpenMedQ is pretrained on 14 fully-open datasets (~3.35M samples) spanning pathology, radiology, microscopy, and text-only clinical QA, enabling systematic study of breadth-of-coverage as a pretraining variable for medical vision-language modeling.

Results: OpenMedQ achieves state-of-the-art BLEU-1 of 75.9 on PathVQA, surpassing Med-PaLM M variants up to 562B parameters (~80× larger), and matches the best reported VQA-MED BLEU-1, demonstrating that data breadth can compensate for scale.

Why it matters: Directly addresses the reader's interests in multimodal learning for medical imaging and vision-language foundation models; the open, reproducible pretraining setup also makes it a strong baseline for radiology report generation research.

2. Masked and Predictive Self-Supervised Foundation Models for 3D Brain MRI

Esra Ergün, Hersh Chandarana, Dan Sodickson, Gözde Ünal  ·  2606.13315

TL;DR: A systematic study of masked and predictive self-supervised pretraining paradigms for 3D brain MRI, specifically targeting disease detection rather than the traditionally studied segmentation tasks.

Problem: Self-supervised foundation models for MRI have been benchmarked almost exclusively on segmentation and dense prediction, leaving their utility for disease detection poorly characterized. It is unclear which SSL paradigm transfers best to detection-oriented clinical tasks.

Method: The authors train and compare two major SSL pretraining families—masked autoencoders and predictive (JEPA-style) models—on large-scale 3D brain MRI corpora, then fine-tune and evaluate on MRI-based disease detection benchmarks to isolate the effect of pretraining strategy.

Results: The investigation reveals clear differences between SSL paradigms for detection tasks, with predictive pretraining showing advantages in capturing diagnostically relevant global structure compared to purely reconstructive masked approaches.

Why it matters: Directly relevant to the reader's core interest in self-supervised learning for medical imaging; provides actionable guidance for choosing SSL pretraining strategies when building radiology foundation models aimed at diagnostic tasks.

3. Hallucination in Medical Imaging AI: A Cross-Modality Analytical Framework for Taxonomy, Detection, and Mitigation under Regulatory Constraints

Omar Alshahrani, Muzammil Behzad  ·  2606.13211

TL;DR: A cross-modality analytical framework that taxonomizes, detects, and proposes mitigations for hallucinations in medical imaging AI—including fabricated anatomy and incorrect laterality in generated radiology reports—under regulatory constraints.

Problem: AI systems in medical imaging are being deployed faster than their failure modes are understood, and hallucination—clinically plausible but factually incorrect outputs such as invented measurements or missed findings in generated reports—poses direct patient safety risks with no unified framework for addressing it.

Method: The paper develops a cross-modality taxonomy of hallucination types in medical imaging AI (covering radiology, pathology, and multimodal settings), surveys detection methods, and maps mitigation strategies to regulatory requirements, providing a structured analytical lens applicable across modalities.

Results: The framework unifies previously fragmented literature on AI errors in medical imaging and identifies systematic gaps in current evaluation and governance approaches, offering concrete recommendations for detection and mitigation that are compatible with emerging regulatory standards.

Why it matters: Highly relevant to the reader's interest in evaluation metrics for generative radiology reports and chest X-ray AI; the hallucination taxonomy directly informs how to design more robust evaluation pipelines and identify failure modes in report generation models.
