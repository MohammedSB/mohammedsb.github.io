1. CORTEX: A Structured Reasoning Benchmark for Trustworthy 3D Chest CT MLLMs

Hashmat Shadab Malik, Anees Ur Rehman Hashmi, Numan Saeed, Muzammal Naseer et al. (6 authors)  ·  2606.27264

Chest CTMedical AI EvaluationMultimodal LLMs

TL;DR: CORTEX is a structured reasoning benchmark for 3D chest CT that requires MLLMs to produce traceable, evidence-grounded diagnoses rather than just final categorical answers.

Problem: Current chest CT QA datasets reduce expert radiological knowledge to simple categorical answers and judge model reasoning only by final output, making it impossible to verify whether a diagnosis is actually grounded in scan evidence. This is especially dangerous in 3D radiology, where findings must trace back to specific anatomical regions.

Method: CORTEX defines a structured benchmark that enforces multi-step, evidence-linked reasoning chains for 3D chest CT MLLMs, using expert radiologist annotations to build QA pairs requiring explicit anatomical evidence citation. Evaluation metrics jointly assess the reasoning chain quality and the final clinical verdict, moving beyond black-box accuracy.

Results: The benchmark exposes significant gaps in current MLLMs' ability to produce trustworthy, verifiable reasoning for 3D chest CT, with models struggling to ground diagnoses in scan evidence when structured intermediate steps are required.

Why it matters: Directly addresses evaluation and trustworthiness of radiology AI—a core challenge for the reader—providing a rigorous framework for assessing multimodal models intended for clinical 3D imaging deployment.

2. TAVR-VLM: Risk-Conditioned Causal Grounding for Hallucination-Resistant Report Generation

Zhixiang Lu, Xiwei Liu, Sifan Song, Changkai Ji et al. (8 authors)  ·  2606.26874

Radiology Report GenerationVision-Language ModelsMedical Imaging

TL;DR: TAVR-VLM uses risk-conditioned causal grounding attention to suppress anatomically ungrounded hallucinations in MLLM-generated TAVR planning reports.

Problem: Applying MLLMs to Transcatheter Aortic Valve Replacement planning is critically impeded by diagnostic hallucinations—generated text that asserts anatomical claims unsupported by the image—which are dangerous in high-stakes cardiac intervention contexts. Standard VLM fine-tuning provides no causal mechanism to anchor text generation to specific image regions.

Method: TAVR-VLM introduces Risk-Conditioned Causal Grounding Attention (R-CGA), which conditions text generation on patient risk scores and enforces causal links between output tokens and spatial anatomical features in the imaging data. This grounding mechanism is integrated into the MLLM inference pipeline without discarding the generative flexibility of the base model.

Results: TAVR-VLM demonstrates improved anatomical grounding and measurably reduced hallucination rates compared to standard MLLM baselines on TAVR planning tasks, producing reports more aligned with expert radiological interpretation.

Why it matters: Directly addresses hallucination and anatomical grounding in medical report generation—core concerns for radiology AI—using a principled causal mechanism applicable beyond TAVR to other high-stakes imaging report generation scenarios.

3. Just how sure are you? Improving Verbalized Uncertainty Calibration in Medical VQA

Eren Senoglu, Federico Toschi, Nicolo Brunello, Andrea Sassella et al. (5 authors)  ·  2606.27023

Medical VQAUncertainty CalibrationMultimodal LLMs

TL;DR: A training framework that calibrates verbalized uncertainty in multimodal LLMs for medical VQA, accounting for the joint visual-textual nature of medical image understanding.

Problem: MLLMs applied to Medical Visual Question Answering consistently overstate confidence regardless of answer correctness, yet existing verbalized confidence calibration methods were designed for text-only LLMs and ignore the visual modality's distinct contribution to uncertainty. Overconfident medical AI outputs are clinically dangerous.

Method: The proposed framework fine-tunes MLLMs with a training objective that jointly optimizes answer correctness and calibrated verbalized confidence, deriving multimodal-specific uncertainty signals from both image and text inputs rather than treating calibration as a purely linguistic problem. The approach requires no architectural changes to the underlying MLLM.

Results: The method significantly improves calibration of verbalized confidence in medical VQA across multiple multimodal LLMs, producing more reliable uncertainty estimates without sacrificing answer accuracy on standard medical imaging benchmarks.

Why it matters: Calibrated uncertainty is essential for safe clinical AI deployment; this work provides the first training framework designed specifically for multimodal medical QA calibration, directly relevant to the reader's interest in evaluation metrics for generative medical AI.

1. ReasonCLIP-58M: Visually Grounded Commonsense Reasoning Supervision for CLIP

Sicheng Zhang, Muzammal Naseer, Binzhu Xie, Naufal Suryanto et al. (8 authors)  ·  2606.26794

Vision-Language ModelsCLIPCommonsense Reasoning

TL;DR: ReasonCLIP-58M introduces a 58M-pair dataset and training strategy that adds visually grounded commonsense reasoning supervision to CLIP, enabling compositional inference beyond descriptive alignment.

Problem: CLIP-style pretraining teaches models to align images with descriptive text but does not supervise commonsense inference or compositional reasoning about visual scenes, limiting their utility as backbones for tasks requiring deeper understanding. It remains unclear whether CLIP-style encoders can support such reasoning without architectural changes.

Method: ReasonCLIP-58M constructs a large-scale dataset of 58M image-text pairs augmented with visually grounded commonsense reasoning annotations, and proposes a training strategy that supervises CLIP encoders with reasoning-grounded contrastive objectives. The approach requires no architectural changes to the standard CLIP dual-encoder structure.

Results: ReasonCLIP-58M substantially improves performance on commonsense reasoning and compositional understanding benchmarks over standard CLIP training, while retaining strong descriptive alignment on established retrieval and classification tasks.

Why it matters: Directly extends the CLIP-style training paradigm—a primary research interest—toward richer visual-semantic understanding; the reasoning supervision approach could benefit medical imaging applications that require clinical inference beyond simple image-text matching.

2. Event-Aware Instructed Assistant for Referring Video Segmentation

Jinyu Liu, Henghui Ding, Shuting He, Yu-Gang Jiang  ·  2606.26994

Referring Video SegmentationVideo UnderstandingFoundation Models

TL;DR: An event-decomposed instructed assistant resolves ambiguity in referring video segmentation by modeling each video as a sequence of distinct temporal events rather than a monolithic sequence of frames.

Problem: Existing referring video segmentation models treat entire videos as a single event, forcing simultaneous parsing of complex multi-event temporal content against a text query, which leads to hallucinations and incorrect grounding—especially for long videos with compositionally rich actions.

Method: The method decomposes a video into temporally distinct events and routes the referring expression to the relevant event segment, using an event-aware instructed assistant architecture that processes each event's content separately before grounding the referring text query. This event decomposition reduces the complexity seen by the segmentation head at any one time.

Results: The event-aware approach achieves state-of-the-art results on referring video segmentation benchmarks, with the largest gains on videos containing multiple distinct temporal events and complex action descriptions.

Why it matters: Referring segmentation is a core research interest; the event decomposition framework offers a principled solution to temporal complexity that generalizes to procedure-level medical video understanding such as surgical phase segmentation with language grounding.

3. DnA: Denoising Attention for Visual Tasks

Ron Campos, Subhajit Maity, Xin Li, Srijan Das et al. (5 authors)  ·  2606.27372

Attention MechanismsVision TransformersSemantic Segmentation

TL;DR: Denoising Attention (DnA) replaces standard softmax attention with a dual positive/negative query mechanism that suppresses irrelevant background features to sharpen visual attention maps.

Problem: Standard softmax attention in vision transformers produces noisy attention patterns that assign non-trivial weights to irrelevant image regions, diluting informative signals and degrading performance on detection and segmentation tasks where precise spatial localization is critical.

Method: DnA adds a negative query alongside the standard positive query in each attention head: the positive query selects class-relevant features while the negative query identifies and suppresses background noise. Their outputs are combined to produce a substantially cleaner attention distribution. This is implemented as a lightweight modification to standard multi-head attention with minimal parameter overhead.

Results: DnA consistently improves performance across multiple visual perception tasks including object detection and semantic segmentation, with measurable gains over standard softmax attention baselines at comparable computational cost.

Why it matters: A broadly applicable attention improvement for vision transformers that could directly enhance any CLIP-style backbone or medical imaging model using transformer architectures, relevant to the reader's foundation model and segmentation interests.
