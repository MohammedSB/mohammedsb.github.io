1. Scalable Training of Spatially Grounded 2D Vision-Language Models for Radiology

Yusuf Salcan, Simon Ging, Robin Schirrmeister, Philipp Arnold et al. (7 authors)  ·  2606.20477

Radiology Vision-Language ModelsVisual GroundingMedical Imaging

TL;DR: A method to train spatially grounded radiology VLMs on CT/MR without manual spatial annotations using LLM-curated VQA and automated segmentation.

Problem: Building visually grounded VLMs for radiology normally requires costly manual spatial (bounding-box/region) annotations, which limits scale and clinical coverage.

Method: The authors introduce RefRad2D, a 1.2M-pair bilingual (German/English) CT/MR image-text dataset with VQA and spatial-grounding subsets generated automatically via LLM-based curation and automated segmentation. They train 2D VLMs on this data to perform grounded reasoning without human spatial labels.

Results: The resulting models achieve spatial grounding and VQA performance derived purely from automatically curated supervision at large scale.

Why it matters: Directly aligned with the reader's work on vision-language foundation models and radiology report generation; the annotation-free grounding pipeline is a transferable recipe for spatially-aware medical VLMs.

2. Confidence Calibration for Multimodal LLMs: An Empirical Study through Medical VQA

Yuetian Du, Yucheng Wang, Ming Kong, Tian Liang et al. (7 authors)  ·  2606.19950

Medical VQAMultimodal LLMsConfidence Calibration

TL;DR: First comprehensive study of the accuracy-confidence relationship in medical MLLMs, with a multi-strategy fusion method to better calibrate elicited confidence.

Problem: Medical MLLMs often express confidence that misaligns with their actual accuracy, risking misdiagnosis or dismissal of correct advice in clinical settings.

Method: The paper empirically analyzes how accuracy and elicited confidence relate across medical MLLMs and proposes a Multi-Strategy Fusion approach that aggregates confidence signals to improve calibration on medical VQA tasks.

Results: The method improves the alignment between model confidence and correctness compared to naive confidence elicitation in medical VQA.

Why it matters: Bears directly on evaluation of generative medical models and trustworthy deployment of VLMs in clinical practice, a core interest of the reader.

3. OTCHA: Optimal Transport-driven Confidence-aware Latent Hub Alignment for Multi-View Medical Image Classification

Jiwoong Yang, Haejun Chung, Ikbeom Jang  ·  2606.19838

Multi-View Medical ImagingOptimal TransportChest Radiography

TL;DR: An optimal-transport-driven, confidence-aware latent hub alignment method for multi-view medical image classification (mammography, chest radiography).

Problem: Multi-view medical images are unregistered and contain view-specific artifacts; naively fusing per-view features lets irrelevant content contaminate the joint representation.

Method: OTCHA aligns per-view latent representations to a shared 'hub' using optimal transport with a confidence-aware weighting scheme, suppressing view-specific noise before fusion. It is evaluated on standard multi-view clinical settings including chest radiography and mammography.

Results: The approach improves multi-view classification over direct fusion baselines by reducing contamination from irrelevant view-specific cues.

Why it matters: Chest radiography multi-view modeling is squarely within the reader's chest X-ray and radiology AI focus, and the OT-based fusion is a reusable multimodal alignment idea.

1. The FID Lottery: Quantifying Hidden Randomness in Generative-Model Evaluation

Nicolas Dufour, Alexei A. Efros, Patrick Pérez  ·  2606.20536

Generative Model EvaluationFIDReproducibility

TL;DR: Treats FID as a random variable across training and sampling seeds and quantifies how much hidden randomness undermines single-number generative-model comparisons.

Problem: FID is the de facto image-generation metric, yet papers report a single number from one trained model and one seed, ignoring its variance under retraining and resampling.

Method: The authors construct a two-axis panel of training and generation seeds, measure FID variance directly, and analyze how reproducible reported scores actually are.

Results: FID exhibits substantial seed-driven variance, implying many reported improvements may fall within noise — a 'FID lottery'.

Why it matters: Evaluation rigor for generative models is directly relevant to the reader's interest in evaluation metrics, and the variance analysis is a cautionary tool for any generative radiology benchmarking.

2. Spectral Query-Key Product Weight Steering for Training-Free VLM Hallucination Mitigation

Karn Tiwari, Varnith Chordia, Prathosh A P  ·  2606.20419

Vision-Language ModelsHallucination MitigationTraining-Free Weight Editing

TL;DR: A data-free, training-free, zero-cost weight edit to the per-head query-key product that reduces object hallucination in VLMs.

Problem: VLMs frequently produce fluent but visually unsupported descriptions, mentioning objects absent from the image.

Method: QK Product Steering directly edits the per-head query-key product (the operator producing pre-softmax attention logits) via spectral suppression, requiring no data, training, or extra inference cost.

Results: The edit reduces object hallucination while preserving generation quality, with no inference-time overhead.

Why it matters: Hallucination control in VLMs matters enormously for trustworthy radiology report generation, and a zero-cost weight edit is appealing for clinical VLM deployment.

3. Variable-Length Tokenization via Learnable Global Merging for Diffusion Transformers

Dong Hoon Lee, Seunghoon Hong  ·  2606.20076

Diffusion TransformersVariable-Length TokenizationImage Tokenizers

TL;DR: A variable-length tokenizer that uses learnable global merging to adaptively set token counts, letting diffusion transformers trade quality against compute.

Problem: Latent diffusion quality-compute trade-offs are locked by a tokenizer's fixed compression ratio, and conventional variable-length tokenizers degrade by truncating ordered tokens.

Method: The method modulates token count via learnable global merging rather than truncation, producing adaptive-length latent representations that diffusion transformers can consume flexibly.

Results: Variable-length tokens allow diffusion models to balance fidelity and compute more effectively than fixed-ratio or truncation-based tokenizers.

Why it matters: Tokenization and diffusion architecture advances are general method contributions relevant to the reader's interest in foundation-model building blocks and efficient generative imaging.
