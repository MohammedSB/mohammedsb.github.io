1. A Multi-Center Benchmark for Abdominal Disease Diagnosis and Report Generation from Non-Contrast CT

Mariam Elbakry, Aliaa Sayed Sheha, Salma Hassan Tantawy, Aya Yassin et al. (8 authors)  ·  2606.16991

TL;DR: A multi-center benchmark for multi-organ abdominal disease diagnosis and automated radiology report generation from non-contrast CT, aiming to reduce reliance on contrast-enhanced CT.

Problem: Multiphasic contrast-enhanced CT is standard for abdominal lesion characterization but carries contrast-induced nephropathy risk, increases acquisition burden, and adds to radiologist workload. There is no standardized resource for diagnosis and report generation from safer non-contrast CT.

Method: The authors introduce a novel multi-center benchmark covering multi-organ abdominal disease diagnosis and automated radiology report generation from non-contrast CT. It provides paired imaging and report data across centers to support training and evaluation of diagnosis and generation models.

Results: The benchmark establishes baselines for disease diagnosis and report generation on non-contrast CT across multiple institutions, enabling standardized comparison.

Why it matters: Directly targets radiology report generation and chest/abdominal imaging AI with multi-center evaluation — squarely in the reader's interests in report generation and evaluation of generative radiology reports.

2. Unified Multimodal Model for Brain MRI Imputation and Understanding

Zhiyun Song, Che Liu, Tian Xia, Avinash Kori et al. (5 authors)  ·  2606.16484

TL;DR: A unified multimodal LLM that jointly performs brain MRI modality imputation and natural-language understanding, addressing scarce data and missing modalities.

Problem: Medical MLLMs inherit knowledge from LLMs and can integrate multiple modalities, but the field is held back by scarce high-quality training data and the frequent occurrence of missing modalities in real-world MRI acquisitions.

Method: The work proposes a unified model that handles both brain MRI imputation (synthesizing missing modalities) and understanding (language-based interpretation) within a single multimodal framework, leveraging LLM knowledge alongside imaging modalities.

Results: The unified approach enables imputation of missing brain MRI modalities while supporting natural-language understanding of the scans, mitigating the missing-data problem.

Why it matters: Combines multimodal foundation-model learning with medical imaging and missing-modality handling — central to the reader's focus on multimodal and self-supervised learning for medical imaging.

3. Uncertainty Is Not a Safety Net for Clinical VQA, but Can It Anticipate Model Failure?

Arnisa Fazla, Alberto Testoni, Ameen Abu-Hanna, Barbara Plank et al. (5 authors)  ·  2606.16583

TL;DR: A systematic study showing current uncertainty estimation methods do not provide a reliable safety net for clinical vision-language models, though they may help anticipate model failure.

Problem: Safe deployment of clinical VLMs requires reliable uncertainty estimation to signal when predictions should be trusted or escalated to a clinician, but it is unclear whether existing methods actually deliver this signal.

Method: The authors benchmark 8 uncertainty estimation methods across 12 vision-language models on clinical visual question answering, analyzing whether uncertainty quality is an intrinsic property and whether it can anticipate failures.

Results: They find uncertainty quality is not an intrinsic property of the method but varies, and that uncertainty is not a dependable safety net — though it shows some capacity to anticipate model failure.

Why it matters: Addresses evaluation, reliability, and uncertainty of clinical VLMs in medical VQA — directly relevant to the reader's interests in medical vision-language models and evaluation metrics.
