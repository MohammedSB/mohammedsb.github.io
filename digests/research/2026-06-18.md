## Top Papers (Full Summary)

### 1. [Beyond Scalar Scores: Exploring LLM-based Metrics for Clinical Significance Evaluation in Radiology Reports](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18797)
**Authors:** Qingyu Lu, Ruochen Li, Liang Ding, Yufei Xia et al. (6 authors)  
**arXiv:** `2606.18797`

**TL;DR:** Proposes LLM-based metrics that judge the clinical significance of generated radiology reports rather than reducing quality to a single ungrounded scalar.

**Problem:** Existing radiology report metrics obscure clinical accuracy by collapsing report quality into a medically ungrounded scalar, which can hide omitted critical findings or mischaracterized observations that directly affect patient care.

**Method:** The work explores LLM-based metrics that explicitly assess clinical significance of findings in generated reports, drawing on the medical knowledge of LLMs while addressing their difficulty in drawing clinically grounded distinctions. It moves evaluation away from scalar scores toward finding-level clinical accuracy judgments.

**Results:** The paper demonstrates that scalar metrics fail to capture clinically critical errors and that LLM-based, significance-aware evaluation better reflects clinical accuracy requirements.

**Why it matters:** Directly targets the reader's interest in evaluation metrics for generative radiology reports — arguably the single most on-topic paper in the batch.


### 2. [Hallucination Detection and Correction in Medical VLMs via Counter-Evidence Verification](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18609)
**Authors:** Nan Zhou, Ke Zou, Meng Liu, Linchao He et al. (8 authors)  
**arXiv:** `2606.18609`

**TL;DR:** A counter-evidence verification framework that detects and corrects hallucinations in medical vision-language models by checking whether attention truly reflects supporting visual evidence.

**Problem:** Medical VLMs produce trust-undermining hallucinations, and existing detection methods focus on text-vs-reference inconsistencies or attention maps without verifying whether attended regions actually provide visual evidence for the claim.

**Method:** The method introduces counter-evidence verification to test whether model attention genuinely reflects the visual evidence supporting a generated statement, enabling both detection and correction of hallucinated content in medical diagnosis settings.

**Results:** The approach improves reliability of medical VLMs by surfacing and correcting hallucinations that prior attention- or text-consistency-based methods miss.

**Why it matters:** Sits squarely at the intersection of vision-language foundation models and radiology AI, addressing reliability — a core concern for the reader's work on medical multimodal models.


### 3. [DINO-Med3D: Bridging Dimension and Domain Gaps in Volumetric Segmentation via Progressive Adaptation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.18886)
**Authors:** Haoyu Hu, Xiyao Ma, Shiqi Liu, Linsen Zhang et al. (7 authors)  
**arXiv:** `2606.18886`

**TL;DR:** DINO-Med3D adapts the pretrained DINOv3 encoder for volumetric medical segmentation via a two-stage progressive framework that bridges 2D-to-3D dimension and domain gaps.

**Problem:** DINOv3 shows strong semantic discrimination on natural images, but direct use for 3D medical segmentation is blocked by both dimensional mismatch (2D vs volumetric) and domain disparity between natural and medical imagery.

**Method:** A two-stage progressive adaptation repurposes the frozen/pretrained DINOv3 encoder for 3D tasks: the first stage mitigates the dimension gap and subsequent adaptation closes the domain gap for volumetric segmentation.

**Results:** The framework enables a natural-image self-supervised foundation model to perform competitive volumetric medical segmentation despite the dimension and domain shifts.

**Why it matters:** Connects self-supervised vision foundation models (DINO family) to medical imaging segmentation, matching the reader's interests in SSL, foundation-model adaptation, and medical CV.
