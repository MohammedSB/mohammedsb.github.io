1. Vision-language models for chest radiography do not always need the image

Mahshad Lotfinia, Sebastian Ziegelmayer, Lisa Adams, Daniel Truhn et al. (6 authors)  ·  2606.17710

TL;DR: A causal audit shows that vision-language models for chest radiography often achieve high accuracy by exploiting finding-name priors rather than actually reading the image.

Problem: Strong chest radiograph accuracy in medical VLMs is widely interpreted as evidence the models use the image, but a model exploiting label priors scores identically to one that reads the scan, and no standard benchmark distinguishes the two.

Method: The authors introduce a causal audit that intervenes on the image — occluding the relevant region, occluding an irrelevant region, and other manipulations — and compares how model predictions change, isolating genuine image use from prior-based shortcutting.

Results: The audit reveals that several VLMs maintain comparable performance even when the diagnostically relevant image region is occluded, indicating reliance on finding-name priors rather than visual evidence.

Why it matters: Directly central to the reader's work on chest X-ray VLMs and evaluation metrics — it exposes a fundamental validity flaw in how radiology VLMs are benchmarked.

2. The Slop Paradox: How Synthetic Standardization Erodes Clinical Uncertainty and Cross-Modal Alignment in AI-Rewritten Radiology Reports

Samar Ansari  ·  2606.17791

TL;DR: A controlled study quantifies how LLM-based rewriting of radiology reports erodes clinical uncertainty and degrades cross-modal alignment with the chest X-ray.

Problem: AI documentation tools increasingly summarize and standardize radiology reports with LLMs, but the resulting information degradation has not been carefully measured.

Method: Using 450 chest X-ray reports from the Indiana University dataset, the authors generate synthetic versions via three realistic LLM rewriting tasks (EHR summarization, standardized rewriting, etc.) and measure information loss, uncertainty erosion, and cross-modal alignment changes.

Results: LLM rewriting systematically flattens clinical uncertainty (the 'slop paradox') and weakens alignment between the report text and the underlying image, degrading clinically meaningful information.

Why it matters: Hits the reader's interests in evaluation metrics for generative radiology reports and chest X-ray analysis, with concrete implications for deploying LLMs in radiology workflows.

3. SegDINO: Introducing Multi-Scale Structure into DINO for Efficient Medical Image Segmentation

Sicheng Yang, Hongqiu Wang, Zhaohu Xing, Sixiang Chen et al. (8 authors)  ·  2606.17972

TL;DR: SegDINO injects multi-scale structure into frozen DINO features to enable efficient, high-performance medical image segmentation without heavy decoders.

Problem: Self-supervised DINO models give strong transferable representations, but applying them to segmentation typically requires heavy decoders with complex upsampling, adding large parameter and compute overhead.

Method: The authors observe that adding scale to DINO features matters far more than decoder capacity, and propose SegDINO, which introduces multi-scale structure into DINO features while keeping the decoder lightweight for efficient medical segmentation.

Results: SegDINO achieves competitive or superior segmentation performance with substantially reduced parameters and computation compared to heavy-decoder approaches.

Why it matters: Squarely matches the reader's focus on self-supervised learning and foundation-model representations for medical imaging segmentation.
