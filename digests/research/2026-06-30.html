1. SHOVIR: A Benchmark for Evaluating Vision Shortcut Learning in Radiology Report Generation

Filippo Ruffini, Marco Salmé, Rosa Sicilia, Valerio Guarrasi et al. (5 authors)  ·  2606.30201

Radiology Report GenerationEvaluation MetricsVision-Language Models

TL;DR: SHOVIR is a benchmark that tests whether radiology report generation models actually ground diagnostic statements in visual evidence rather than exploiting dataset shortcuts.

Problem: Standard RRG metrics measure report-level lexical overlap or aggregate clinical correctness, which lets models score well by exploiting learned priors and frequent findings without truly attending to image pathology. There is no protocol to check whether each diagnostic statement stems from the actual visible evidence.

Method: The authors build SHOVIR, a benchmark and evaluation protocol that isolates vision shortcut learning in VLMs for RRG, testing whether individual diagnostic statements are causally tied to the pathological evidence in the image. They evaluate VLM-based report generators against this statement-level grounding criterion rather than only report-level scores.

Results: Models that achieve competitive conventional scores are shown to rely substantially on shortcuts, revealing a gap between reported metrics and genuine visual grounding.

Why it matters: This sits squarely at the intersection of the reader's interests in radiology report generation and evaluation metrics for generative reports, offering a tool to expose and quantify a failure mode that standard metrics hide.

2. Data-Efficient Multimodal Alignment for Histopathology-based Molecular Prediction

Dominik Winter, Dominik Vonficht, Loïc Le Bescond, Christian Gebbe et al. (9 authors)  ·  2606.29949

HistopathologyMultimodal LearningFoundation Models

TL;DR: A lightweight alignment module on top of frozen histopathology and RNA-seq foundation models enables open-vocabulary molecular prompting from H&E whole-slide images.

Problem: H&E whole-slide images are abundant and spatially rich but lack molecular specificity, while bulk RNA-seq is transcriptome-wide but costly and rarely archived. Bridging the two data-efficiently is a key challenge for scalable molecular pathology.

Method: The authors train only a small alignment module that connects frozen pathology and RNA-seq foundation-model embeddings into a shared space. This enables querying H&E slides with arbitrary molecular prompts (open-vocabulary molecular prediction) without retraining the large backbones.

Results: The data-efficient alignment yields open-vocabulary molecular prediction from histology, demonstrating that frozen foundation-model representations can be cheaply coupled across modalities.

Why it matters: Directly relevant to the reader's focus on self-supervised and multimodal learning for medical imaging and on vision-language-style foundation models applied in pathology.

3. Uncertainty Estimation in Pathology Foundation Models via Deep Mutual Learning

Gbègninougbo Aurel Davy Tchokponhoue, Sevda Öğüt, Ali Idri, Dorina Thanou et al. (5 authors)  ·  2606.30020

HistopathologyFoundation ModelsUncertainty Estimation

TL;DR: DICE is a plug-and-play deep mutual learning framework that adds reliable uncertainty estimates to pathology foundation models for whole-slide image analysis.

Problem: Pathology foundation models give generalizable WSI representations but lack trustworthy confidence estimates, and no single model is best across all tasks, undermining clinical adoption.

Method: The authors propose DICE, a deep mutual learning framework that combines multiple pathology foundation models so they teach each other, producing calibrated uncertainty estimates while leveraging complementary strengths. It is designed as a plug-and-play layer atop existing PFMs.

Results: DICE improves uncertainty quantification and robustness across tasks relative to relying on any individual foundation model.

Why it matters: Speaks to the reader's interest in medical-imaging foundation models and the practical evaluation/reliability concerns needed for clinical deployment.

1. Open-Vocabulary and Referring Segmentation for 3D Gaussians Using 2D Detectors

Jameel Hassan, Yasiru Ranasinghe, Vishal Patel  ·  2606.30638

Referring SegmentationOpen-Vocabulary Segmentation3D Gaussian Splatting

TL;DR: A method brings open-vocabulary and referring segmentation to 3D Gaussian Splatting scenes by distilling 2D detector and CLIP features into per-Gaussian semantics.

Problem: Extending 3DGS scene reconstruction with language-driven, open-vocabulary understanding is needed for embodied AI, but learning high-dimensional CLIP features per Gaussian is expensive and noisy.

Method: The approach assigns language-grounded semantics to 3D Gaussians by distilling features from off-the-shelf 2D detectors and CLIP, supporting both open-vocabulary and referring (text-conditioned) segmentation of the reconstructed scene without dense 3D annotation.

Results: The method enables text-driven and open-vocabulary segmentation directly on 3D Gaussian scenes using only 2D detectors as supervision.

Why it matters: Referring segmentation and CLIP-style vision-language grounding are core reader interests; this shows a clean transfer of those ideas into 3D reconstruction.

2. Same Concept, Different Directions: Cross-Modal Feature Heterogeneity in Sparse Autoencoders

Chungpa Lee, Jihoon Kwon, Kyle Min, Jy-yong Sohn  ·  2606.29888

Vision-Language ModelsSparse AutoencodersInterpretability

TL;DR: Sparse autoencoders reveal that the same concept is encoded along different directions for image and text in CLIP-style joint embedding spaces.

Problem: Vision-language models map images and text into a joint space, but those embeddings entangle multiple features and are hard to interpret; whether shared concepts use shared directions across modalities was untested.

Method: The authors apply sparse autoencoders to joint VLM embeddings and explicitly test the implicit assumption that a concept occupies the same direction for both modalities, characterizing cross-modal feature heterogeneity.

Results: They find systematic cross-modal heterogeneity—identical concepts point in different directions for vision and language—challenging assumptions behind controllability and interpretability of joint embeddings.

Why it matters: Bears directly on the reader's interest in CLIP-style vision-language foundation models and self-supervised/contrastive representation analysis.

3. Nemotron-Labs-Diffusion-Image: Advancing Masked Discrete Diffusion for High-Resolution Image Synthesis

Shufan Li, Greg Heinrich, Hanrong Ye, Yonggan Fu et al. (7 authors)  ·  2606.29814

Diffusion ModelsText-to-Image GenerationFoundation Models

TL;DR: Nemotron-Labs-Diffusion-Image is a state-of-the-art masked discrete diffusion model for high-resolution text-to-image synthesis.

Problem: Unlike continuous latent diffusion, standard masked discrete diffusion models lack the global self-consistency needed for high-resolution image generation, limiting their competitiveness.

Method: The authors design a masked discrete diffusion model that addresses self-consistency and high-resolution synthesis challenges of prior masked image generation, advancing the discrete-token generative paradigm for text-to-image.

Results: The model reaches state-of-the-art quality for high-resolution masked discrete diffusion image synthesis.

Why it matters: A notable advance in generative foundation models and diffusion architectures, relevant for the reader's broader interest in generative methods and self-supervised vision modeling.
